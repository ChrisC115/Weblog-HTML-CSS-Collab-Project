Schrödinger's Cat Thought Experiment

Schrödinger's cat is a famous thought experiment, not a real one, designed by physicist Erwin Schrödinger in 1935 to highlight the perceived absurdity of applying quantum mechanics principles, specifically superposition, to macroscopic objects.

In the scenario, a hypothetical cat is placed in a sealed box with a device containing a tiny amount of radioactive substance, a Geiger counter, and a vial of poison. The setup is such that if a single atom decays (a random quantum event), the Geiger counter triggers a mechanism that shatters the poison vial, killing the cat.

According to the Copenhagen interpretation of quantum mechanics, until an observer opens the box and looks inside, the radioactive atom is in a superposition of both decayed and undecayed states simultaneously. Because the cat's fate is linked to this quantum event, the interpretation implies the cat is also in a superposition, meaning it is both alive and dead at the same time.

The paradox emphasizes the question of when a quantum system "collapses" into a single, definite reality (either a living or a dead cat), as our everyday intuition tells us the cat must be one or the other, even when unobserved.
